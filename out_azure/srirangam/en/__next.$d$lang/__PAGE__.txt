14:T1ee0,Sri Ranganathaswamy Temple, Srirangam
The First and Foremost of the 108 Divya Desams
Located on the sacred island of Srirangam (Thiruvarangam) in Tiruchirappalli District, Tamil Nadu, the Arulmigu Sri Ranganathaswamy Temple is the foremost among the 108 Divya Desams glorified by the Azhwars. Revered as "Bhooloka Vaikuntam" (Heaven on Earth) and affectionately known as the "Periya Koil" (The Great Temple), it is one of the largest functioning Hindu temples in the world and stands as the spiritual heart of Sri Vaishnavism.
Every one of the twelve Azhwars has sung in praise of Lord Ranganatha and the unparalleled greatness of this sacred shrine. It is also the holy place where Sri Andal, Thiruppaan Azhwar, and Thondaradippodi Azhwar attained the divine feet of Lord Ranganatha.
Situated approximately 5 km north of Tiruchirappalli, 330 km south of Chennai, 130 km north of Madurai, and 200 km east of Coimbatore, the temple is well connected by road, rail, and air, making it easily accessible to devotees from across India and the world.

Temple Architecture and Layout
The temple is an architectural masterpiece spread over approximately 156 acres, making it one of the largest temple complexes in Asia. The sacred complex consists of seven concentric enclosures (Saptha Prakaras or Thiruchutrus), symbolizing the seven celestial realms through which devotees progress towards the divine.
The sanctum of Lord Ranganatha is situated within the innermost five enclosures:
- Thiruvannazhi Thiruchutru 
- Rajamahendran Thiruchutru 
- Kulasekaran Thiruchutru 
- Alinadan Thiruchutru 
- Agalangan Thiruchutru 

The outer two enclosures are:
- Thiruvikraman Thiruchutru (Uthara Veedhi) 
- Kalikandan Thiruchutru (Chithirai Veedhi) 

Within these seven enclosures stand 21 majestic gopurams and 4 unfinished (Mottai) gopurams.
The most prominent among them is the magnificent Southern Rajagopuram, which rises to an impressive height of 236 feet (72 metres) with 13 storeys and 13 kalasams. Although construction began nearly 400 years ago during the Nayak period, it remained incomplete until 1987, when it was completed under the guidance and tireless efforts of the 44th Jeeyar of Ahobila Mutt, Srimad Azhagiyasingar. It continues to be one of the tallest temple towers in Asia.
Situated on the banks of the sacred River Cauvery, whose waters are regarded as even more sanctifying than the Ganges according to temple tradition, Srirangam occupies a unique place in Hindu spirituality.

The Mulavar – Sri Ranganatha Perumal
The Moolavar (Presiding Deity) of Srirangam is Sri Ranganatha Swamy, one of the most magnificent and unique forms of Lord Maha Vishnu.
The Lord is enshrined in the sanctum in the Bhujanga Sayanam posture—reclining majestically on the thousand-hooded serpent Adisesha, facing south toward Lanka, fulfilling the promise made to Vibhishana, the righteous brother of Ravana.
Unlike standing or seated forms found in many Vishnu temples, Sri Ranganatha is seen resting gracefully, symbolizing the Lord's eternal protection of the universe while remaining in divine yogic repose.
The Moolavar possesses several unique characteristics:
- He faces south, an extremely rare orientation among Vishnu temples. 
- His divine chest bears the sacred Kaustubha gem, representing His supreme status as the Lord of Vaikunta. 
- The Lord wears the ever-present Kasturi Tilakam on His forehead. 
- He holds the eternal Sengol (Royal Sceptre), signifying His role as the righteous ruler of the universe. 
- His incomparable beauty earned Him the name Azhagiya Manavalan ("The Beautiful Divine Bridegroom"). 

Temple tradition reveres this deity as one possessing exceptional glory in all three sacred aspects:
- Sthalam (the holy place) 
- Moorthy (the presiding deity) 
- Theertham (the sacred waters) 

The temple's sacred Surya Pushkarani is believed to have been worshipped by Surya Bhagavan, making it one of the Navagraha-related sacred waters.

The Utsavar – Namperumal
The Utsava Murthy of Srirangam is Sri Namperumal, perhaps the most celebrated festival deity in the entire Sri Vaishnava tradition.
While the Moolavar remains permanently enshrined within the sanctum, Namperumal comes out in grand processions throughout the year, blessing devotees inside and outside the temple. For this reason, devotees often say:
"In Srirangam, the temple belongs to Namperumal."
Namperumal is adorned daily in exquisite silk garments, fragrant flower garlands, rare jewels, and magnificent crowns. Each day brings a unique alankaram (decoration), reflecting different divine forms and moods.
He presides over almost every important temple festival, including:
- Vaikunta Ekadasi 
- Panguni Uthiram 
- Chithirai Brahmotsavam 
- Vasanthotsavam 
- Jyeshtabhishekam 
- Pavithrotsavam 
- Navaratri 
- Adhyayana Utsavam 

During these festivals, Namperumal is taken through the seven prakaras and the streets of Srirangam on magnificent vahanas such as:
- Garuda Vahanam 
- Hanuman Vahanam 
- Sesha Vahanam 
- Horse Vahanam 
- Elephant Vahanam 
- Golden Palanquin 
- Golden Chariot 
- Surya Prabhai 
- Chandra Prabhai 

Among all His festivals, the Vaikunta Ekadasi procession is considered the grandest, attracting hundreds of thousands of devotees from across the world.

Viswaroopa Seva – The Divine Awakening
One of the most unique daily rituals at Srirangam is the Viswaroopa Seva, performed every morning at approximately 6:15 a.m.
Before sunrise, the temple elephant brings sacred water from the Northern Cauvery (Kollidam) for the Lord's worship. During the Tamil month of Aippasi, water is ceremonially brought from the Southern Cauvery near Amma Mandapam.
As part of this beautiful tradition, the temple elephant, horse, and a sacred cow are brought before the sanctum. Their presence symbolizes the entire creation awakening the Lord to begin another day of blessing the universe. The gentle sounds of Vedic chanting, temple bells, conches, and devotional hymns fill the sanctum as the doors are opened for the first darshan of the day.
Devotees consider witnessing Viswaroopa Seva to be highly auspicious, believing that it grants spiritual merit, removes obstacles, and invokes the Lord's divine grace at the very beginning of the day.

A Living Centre of Sri Vaishnavism
Srirangam is not merely an architectural marvel or an ancient pilgrimage destination—it is the spiritual headquarters of the Sri Vaishnava Sampradaya.
The temple is deeply associated with Bhagavad Ramanujacharya, who lived, taught, and established the principles of Vishishtadvaita Vedanta here. His sacred shrine, containing his preserved physical form, continues to inspire millions of devotees.
For centuries, the temple has preserved an unbroken tradition of:
- Vedic chanting 
- Divya Prabandham recitation 
- Agama worship 
- Temple music 
- Annual festivals 
- Ritual processions 

These traditions continue to be practiced exactly as prescribed by the ancient Acharyas, making Srirangam a living embodiment of devotion, philosophy, culture, and temple heritage.

Sri Ranganathaswamy Temple at Srirangam is far more than a magnificent monument. It is a timeless spiritual kingdom where devotion, history, architecture, music, literature, and philosophy converge. From the serene majesty of Sri Ranganatha, reclining eternally on Adisesha, to the vibrant festival processions of Sri Namperumal, every ritual reflects centuries of uninterrupted worship and profound faith.
Revered as Bhooloka Vaikuntam, Srirangam continues to draw millions of devotees seeking the divine grace of Lord Ranganatha. The Sudarshana Chakra — Puranic Background
The Sudarshana Chakra is the divine weapon of Sri Mahavishnu. It is a spinning, disc-shaped weapon with 108 serrated edges. Among Vishnu's four divine hands, the Sudarshana Chakra is held in the right rear hand, the conch Panchajanyam in the left fore hand, the mace Kaumodaki in the left rear hand, and the lotus bud (Padmam) in the right fore hand.

Origin
Sanjna Devi, the wife of Surya the Sun God, was the daughter of Vishwakarma, the divine architect. Unable to bear the Sun's intense radiance and heat, she could not approach her husband and appealed to her father for help. Vishwakarma placed the Sun on his grinding wheel and trimmed away the excess brilliance. From the solar dust that fell away, he fashioned three wondrous creations: the first was the Pushpaka Vimana, the famed aerial chariot (later belonging to Kubera and then to Ravana); the second was the Trishula (Trident) of Lord Shiva; and the third was the Sudarshana Chakra of Lord Vishnu. The Chakra carries millions of spike-like serrations arranged in two rows that spin in opposite directions, giving it its fearsome cutting edge.

Who is Lord Dhanvantri?
According to Hindu theology, Dhanvantri is an avatar of Lord Vishnu, manifesting as the physician of the Devas (gods). His origin story is found in the Puranas, primarily during the grand cosmic event known as Samudra Manthan (the Churning of the Ocean of Milk).
When the Devas and Asuras churned the cosmic ocean using Mount Mandara as the churning rod and the serpent Vasuki as the rope, many divine treasures emerged. Among the last to appear was Lord Dhanvantri. He arose from the milky waves holding a kalasha (pot) filled with Amrita (the nectar of immortality) in one hand, and a leech (Jalauka) in another, along with the Sudarshana Chakra and the Shankha (conch).

The Mortal Incarnation
According to the Vishnu Purana, Dhanvantri incarnated on earth as the King of Kashi (Varanasi). Through his great-grandson Divodasa, Ayurvedic knowledge was transmitted to the world. The foundational texts of Ayurveda — the Charaka Samhita and the Sushruta Samhita — honour him as the primal teacher of the healing sciences.

The Shrine at Srirangam — Location
The fourth prakaram (enclosure) of the Srirangam temple is known as Mahar Lokam. Its walls were built by Thirumangai Azhwar. This enclosure also houses the enormous Garuda Mandapam with over 200 pillars — perhaps the most magnificent mandapam in the entire complex — and the Paramapada Vaasal (Gateway to Heaven) on its southern side, which opens only once a year on Vaikunta Ekadasi.
The Dhanvantri Sannidhi stands on an elevated platform on the western side of the Chandra Pushkarani (temple tank), facing east. It is one of only two shrines in the entire complex that are raised above ground level — the other being the Mettazhagiya Singar (Narasimha) shrine.
Temple tradition holds that on the path leading to the Thayar (Ranganayaki) shrine, Sriman Narayana himself appears as Dhanvantri to serve as the physician of Lord Ranganatha.

- Upper right hand — Chakra (Sudarshana discus)
- Upper left hand — Shankha (conch)
- Lower right hand — Amrita Kalasha (pot of nectar)
- Lower left hand — Varada Mudra (the gesture of bestowing boons)

Exceptional Features by Agama Standards
Two features make this shrine architecturally unique within the temple:
**No Utsavar.** Unlike virtually every other sannidhi in the complex, this shrine has no utsava bera — no processional deity. The Moolavar alone receives worship.
**No Vimana.** By Agama convention, every shrine in a temple should have a vimana (tower structure) above the sanctum. The Dhanvantri Sannidhi has none. The traditional explanation is characteristically practical: Dhanvantri is a physician; patients come to him. A vimana is therefore not required. This is one of the most distinctive Agamic exceptions in all of Srirangam.

Historical Evidence — The Inscription
The Hoysala-Era Foundation
In front of the shrine stands a stone slab bearing an inscription catalogued as ARE 80 of 1936-37 (Annual Report on Epigraphy). It dates from the third regnal year of the Hoysala king Pratapachakravartin Vira Ramanatha Deva (r. 1255–1295 CE) and records the following:
- **Patron:** Changadeva Singanna Dandanayaka, pradhani (chief minister) of the king
- **Custodian:** Garudavahana Pandita (Garudavahana Bhattar), the royal physician, designated as the rakshaka (protector) of the donor
- **What was founded:** A salai (hospital) to the west of the north gopuram in the fourth prakaram, with the enshrinement of the deity "Eduttakai-alagiya Nayinar"
- **Land endowment:** Agricultural land in Mummudisola Chaturvedimangalam, in Vilanadu, a subdivision of Pandikulasani Valanadu, was granted for the upkeep of the hospital
The inscription confirms that Garudavahana Bhattar — a practising Ayurvedic physician — established the Dhanvantri idol within the temple. This is not oral tradition; it is epigraphic record.

The Arogyashala — An Ancient Hospital
In earlier centuries, the Dhanvantri Sannidhi was not merely a place of worship. It functioned as the arogyashala — the hospital — for the residents of Srirangam. This is a significant detail for understanding the social role of South Indian temples: they were not only centres of worship and learning but also institutions of public health. The Dhanvantri shrine at Srirangam is physical evidence of this tradition.

Destruction and Rebuilding
The shrine was damaged during the devastating raids of Malik Kafur (1311 CE) and Ulugh Khan (1323 CE), during which much of the temple complex was plundered. It was subsequently rebuilt during the Nayak period. The painted ceilings and decorative elements visible today are attributed to the Nayak-era restoration.

Dhanvantri and Ranganatha — Special Rituals
The Kashayam — Divine Medical Service
One of the most charming traditions associated with this shrine dates from the time of Ramanuja (11th–12th century). Every day, before the aravanai — the final puja of the day when the Lord retires — a medicinal kashayam (herbal decoction) was sent from the Dhanvantri shrine to Lord Ranganatha.
The logic behind this practice is disarmingly simple: Ranganatha receives continuous offerings of very rich foods throughout the day — pongal, tamarind rice, sweet pongal, payasam, and many other preparations. To keep the Lord's system healthy after such a diet, Dhanvantri — as the resident physician — would dispatch a kashayam as his medical service to the Lord. A digestive made from ginger and jaggery was also sent to Ranganatha after certain meals.
This practice transforms an abstract theological concept — Dhanvantri as the physician of the gods — into a daily, tangible ritual within the temple's routine.

Herbal Theertham for Devotees
Visitors to the shrine receive a prasada theertham — a herbal decoction — as a sacred offering. This continues the ancient association between the shrine and healing, and distinguishes it from every other sannidhi in the complex, where the prasadam is invariably food or sanctified water.

Devotees suffering from illness — particularly chronic or otherwise intractable ailments — come to this shrine to perform archana and thirumanjanam before Dhanvantri Perumal, praying for relief. It is a deeply held belief that worship here can cure bodily afflictions and restore health and vitality.
Dhanvantri Jayanti is celebrated annually on Dhanteras (Dhanvantri Trayodashi) — the thirteenth lunar day of Krishna Paksha in the month of Kartik, one day before Diwali. This day is also observed as National Ayurveda Day across India.

While Lord Ranganatha is revered as the Supreme Lord reclining on Adisesha, it is Sri Ranga Nayaki who embodies His infinite compassion, grace, and motherly protection. The Sri Ranga Nayaki Temple is unique among all Divya Desams. The Supreme Mother of Sri Vaishnavism

In Sri Vaishnava philosophy, salvation is possible through the combined grace of Divine Mother (Purushakara) and Lord Narayana. The Acharyas explain that while Lord Narayana is the Supreme Judge, Sri Mahalakshmi is the embodiment of mercy. She listens to the prayers of devotees, softens the Lord's justice with compassion, and recommends every sincere soul for His divine grace.

This theological principle reaches its highest expression at Srirangam.

Sri Vedanta Desika beautifully explains that no devotee should approach the Lord without first seeking the blessings of Thayar. Likewise, Sri Ramanuja consistently emphasized that surrender (Prapatti) becomes complete only through the compassionate mediation of Sri Mahalakshmi.

For this reason, countless devotees visit Sri Ranga Nayaki before or immediately after receiving darshan of Sri Ranganatha.

Why She is Called "Ranga Nayaki"

The name Ranga Nayaki literally means "Queen of Srirangam".

Ranga refers to Sri Rangam, the sacred abode of Lord Ranganatha.

Nayaki means Queen, Divine Mistress, or Supreme Lady.

She is regarded as the sovereign ruler of the spiritual kingdom of Bhooloka Vaikuntham. While Lord Ranganatha is the king, temple traditions often describe Sri Ranga Nayaki as the compassionate queen who governs with infinite mercy.

Even today many devotees affectionately address her as:

- Sri Ranga Nachiyar

- Periya Piratti

- Thayar

- Amma

- Mahalakshmi of Srirangam

The word "Thayar", meaning "Mother," perfectly reflects her relationship with devotees.

Historical Development of the Shrine

The shrine of Sri Ranga Nayaki evolved over several centuries under the patronage of the Cholas, Pandyas, Hoysalas, Vijayanagara rulers, and Nayak kings. Temple chronicles known as the Koil Olugu describe the growth of the temple, royal endowments, festivals, jewels, and ritual traditions connected with both Lord Ranganatha and Thayar.

Unlike many temples where the Goddess occupies a comparatively smaller shrine, Srirangam's architects created an independent temple for Thayar with its own sanctum, mandapas, processional routes, and festivals. This architectural choice reflects the elevated theological status accorded to Mahalakshmi within Sri Vaishnavism.

Several inscriptions record donations of gold ornaments, silk garments, lamps, lands, and villages dedicated specifically for the worship of Sri Ranga Nayaki.

The Unique Tradition – Thayar Rarely Leaves Her Temple

One of the most fascinating traditions at Srirangam is that Sri Ranga Nayaki almost never leaves her sanctum.

Unlike Namperumal (the processional form of Lord Ranganatha), who participates in numerous festivals throughout the year, Thayar generally remains within her shrine. This practice symbolizes the dignity and majesty of the Divine Queen.

During important festivals, the Lord Himself comes to visit His Divine Consort rather than the Goddess travelling outside.

This beautiful tradition teaches that while the Lord actively seeks His devotees, the Divine Mother remains the ever-compassionate refuge where every soul may approach without hesitation.

Panguni Uttiram – The Divine Wedding

The grandest festival associated with Sri Ranga Nayaki is Panguni Uttiram, celebrated during the Tamil month of Panguni (March–April).

This festival commemorates the celestial wedding of Lord Ranganatha and Sri Ranga Nayaki. It is among the most emotionally moving celebrations in the temple calendar.

Thousands of devotees gather to witness the sacred union, believing that participation brings harmony in marriage, family happiness, prosperity, and spiritual blessings.

The festival also symbolizes the inseparable union of Sri (Lakshmi) and Narayana, representing compassion and justice, grace and protection, divine love and cosmic order.

The Koil Olugu and temple traditions preserve detailed accounts of these ceremonies and their historical continuity across centuries.

Sri Vaishnava philosophy identifies Mahalakshmi with six principal qualities:

- Compassion

- Mercy

- Prosperity

- Divine Wisdom

- Protection

- Mediation before the Lord

Acharyas explain that even when devotees feel unworthy of approaching the Supreme Lord, they may confidently approach Thayar, who never rejects anyone.

This doctrine of Purushakara is one of the defining theological contributions of the Sri Vaishnava tradition and is deeply associated with Srirangam.

Sri Ramanuja and Ranga Nayaki

Sri Ramanuja regarded Srirangam as the spiritual capital of Sri Vaishnavism. During his long residence here, he systematized temple worship, festivals, and administrative practices.

The daily rituals established under his guidance ensured that worship of Sri Ranga Nayaki received equal prominence alongside Lord Ranganatha. His reforms profoundly influenced temple life and are reflected in later temple records.
