In the Krita Yuga he served as Adisesha, the serpent-couch of Sri Ranganatha; in the Treta Yuga as Lakshmana, the younger brother of Sri Rama; in the Dvapara Yuga as Balarama, the elder brother of Krishna; and in the Kali Yuga he incarnated as Sri Ramanuja. To reform the world, to reveal the path of bhakti and sharanagati (surrender), and to lead humanity towards salvation, Sri Mannarayana in Paramapada summoned Adisesha and commanded him: "Live in the mortal world for 200 years and carry out the work described above." It was for this reason that he took birth as Ramanuja.
At Thiruvarangam, the Lord (Perumal), through Alavandhar's son Thiruvarangap Perumal Araiyar, arranged to bring Ramanuja, who was then in Kanchipuram, to Srirangam. Previously, five classes of temple servants carried out the koyil kainkaryam (temple service). They were the Kovanavar, Kodavar, Koduval Eduppar, Paduvaar and Thazhaiiduvaar. Ramanuja, during his time, expanded these five into ten groups. Following the Parameshwara Samhita of the Pancharatra Agama and the Thenkalai sampradaya, Ramanuja reorganised and refined the conduct of daily worship (thiruvaradhanam), sannidhi festivals and utsavams. In addition to the ten groups of Brahmin temple servants, he also appointed Vaishnava kainkaryaparas such as Ekangis and Sathatha Mudalis, and ensured the efficient administration of temple services.
The maximum lifespan of a human being has been reckoned at 123 years. Ramanuja lived for 120 years. In 1137 CE, at the time of his passing, he laid his head in the lap of his younger brother and disciple Embar, and placed his sacred feet in the lap of his disciple Andhranirnan, and thus departed this world to fill the seat that had long remained vacant beside the Lord in Paramapada.
Namperumal appeared in a dream and commanded that Ramanuja's mortal form (thirumeni) should be laid to rest in His own Vasantha Mandapam within the Akalangan Thiruchutru (enclosure). This was done accordingly. That place is today known as the Udaiyavar Sannidhi (Ramanuja's shrine). After Ramanuja's body was interred, a form manifested on its own — and that self-manifested form is the Moolavar (presiding deity) of the Ramanuja Sannidhi today. Ramanuja continues to watch over the administration he established, ensuring it is carried on without interruption.
This Moolavar of Ramanuja does not receive thirumanjanam (ceremonial ablution). Instead, a mixture of saffron (kungumappu) and green camphor (pachhai karpooram) is applied to the thirumeni twice a year — once in the month of Aippasi and once in Chithirai. In this manner, without any chemical preservative, through entirely natural means using saffron and green camphor, the sacred body is preserved from decay at this temple.
Ramanuja also served as the administrative reformer at Tirumala Tirupati, increasing the temple's endowments and performing many kainkaryams there. At present, in recognition of this service, the Srirangam temple's Jeer — the Sri Ranganaryana Jeer, who heads the Ramanuja Matham at Srirangam — has been accorded the Sri Sadari honour at Tirumala Tirupati annually since 2003. On that day, the Sri Sadari of Lord Tiruvengadamudaiyan is brought out to the Mugathuvaaram and the honour is formally conferred upon the Jeer, who then has darshan.
Furthermore, at the request of this temple's administration, the Tirumala Tirupati Devasthanam, acting under Andhra Pradesh Government Order No. 164 dated 7-11-2006, and as per the letter of the Commissioner of the Hindu Religious and Charitable Endowments Department, Chennai (No. 52664/2006 dated 08.09.2006), allotted 5,000 square feet of land at Tirumala Tirupati at an annual rent of Rs. 1,000 for a period of 30 years, on which a Jeer Matham with accommodation facilities has been constructed by the Joint Commissioner/Executive Officer.

The deity enshrined in the Sri Chakkarathazhwar Sannidhi is known as Sudarsana Azhwar. He appears with eight divine hands, holding the conch (shanku), discus (chakram) and goad (angusam) among his attributes. Behind him, the imprint of the chakra emblem can be seen. Devotees believe that worshipping him bestows many benefits. Every Saturday, large numbers of devotees visit and light ghee lamps (nei vilakku) as an offering. The Sudarshana Chakra — Puranic Background
The Sudarshana Chakra is the divine weapon of Sri Mahavishnu. It is a spinning, disc-shaped weapon with 108 serrated edges. Among Vishnu's four divine hands, the Sudarshana Chakra is held in the right rear hand, the conch Panchajanyam in the left fore hand, the mace Kaumodaki in the left rear hand, and the lotus bud (Padmam) in the right fore hand.

Origin
Sanjna Devi, the wife of Surya the Sun God, was the daughter of Vishwakarma, the divine architect. Unable to bear the Sun's intense radiance and heat, she could not approach her husband and appealed to her father for help. Vishwakarma placed the Sun on his grinding wheel and trimmed away the excess brilliance. From the solar dust that fell away, he fashioned three wondrous creations: the first was the Pushpaka Vimana, the famed aerial chariot (later belonging to Kubera and then to Ravana); the second was the Trishula (Trident) of Lord Shiva; and the third was the Sudarshana Chakra of Lord Vishnu. The Chakra carries millions of spike-like serrations arranged in two rows that spin in opposite directions, giving it its fearsome cutting edge.

The Sudarshana Chakra in the Puranas
The Sudarshana Chakra is referenced in the Rig Veda, the Yajur Veda and the Puranas as the ultimate weapon for the protection of dharma — deployed against rakshasas, asuras and those who walk the path of adharma.
In the Mahabharata, during Emperor Yudhishthira's Rajasuya Yagna, Sri Krishna employed the Sudarshana Chakra to slay Shishupala, who had committed a hundred offences each worthy of death. The Chakra was also used to shape the Mandara mountain for the Churning of the Ocean of Milk (Samudra Manthan).
When Sati Devi, the consort of Lord Shiva, gave up her life at her father Daksha's sacrificial fire, Shiva, consumed by grief, carried her lifeless body and wandered inconsolably. Dedicated to Lord Dhanvantri — the divine physician and the god of Ayurveda — this shrine is not merely a place of worship but a testament to ancient India's integration of spiritual faith and physical healing.

Who is Lord Dhanvantri?
According to Hindu theology, Dhanvantri is an avatar of Lord Vishnu, manifesting as the physician of the Devas (gods). His origin story is found in the Puranas, primarily during the grand cosmic event known as Samudra Manthan (the Churning of the Ocean of Milk).
When the Devas and Asuras churned the cosmic ocean using Mount Mandara as the churning rod and the serpent Vasuki as the rope, many divine treasures emerged. Among the last to appear was Lord Dhanvantri. He arose from the milky waves holding a kalasha (pot) filled with Amrita (the nectar of immortality) in one hand, and a leech (Jalauka) in another, along with the Sudarshana Chakra and the Shankha (conch).

The Mortal Incarnation
According to the Vishnu Purana, Dhanvantri incarnated on earth as the King of Kashi (Varanasi). Through his great-grandson Divodasa, Ayurvedic knowledge was transmitted to the world. The foundational texts of Ayurveda — the Charaka Samhita and the Sushruta Samhita — honour him as the primal teacher of the healing sciences.

The Shrine at Srirangam — Location
The fourth prakaram (enclosure) of the Srirangam temple is known as Mahar Lokam. Its walls were built by Thirumangai Azhwar. This enclosure also houses the enormous Garuda Mandapam with over 200 pillars — perhaps the most magnificent mandapam in the entire complex — and the Paramapada Vaasal (Gateway to Heaven) on its southern side, which opens only once a year on Vaikunta Ekadasi.
The Dhanvantri Sannidhi stands on an elevated platform on the western side of the Chandra Pushkarani (temple tank), facing east. It is one of only two shrines in the entire complex that are raised above ground level — the other being the Mettazhagiya Singar (Narasimha) shrine.
Temple tradition holds that on the path leading to the Thayar (Ranganayaki) shrine, Sriman Narayana himself appears as Dhanvantri to serve as the physician of Lord Ranganatha.

The Deity
The Moolavar (presiding deity) is a standing image (sthanaka thirukkolam) of Vishnu in his Dhanvantri form, approximately 5 feet in height, with four arms:
- Upper right hand — Chakra (Sudarshana discus)
- Upper left hand — Shankha (conch)
- Lower right hand — Amrita Kalasha (pot of nectar)
- Lower left hand — Varada Mudra (the gesture of bestowing boons)

Exceptional Features by Agama Standards
Two features make this shrine architecturally unique within the temple:
**No Utsavar.** Unlike virtually every other sannidhi in the complex, this shrine has no utsava bera — no processional deity. The Moolavar alone receives worship.
**No Vimana.** By Agama convention, every shrine in a temple should have a vimana (tower structure) above the sanctum. The Dhanvantri Sannidhi has none. The traditional explanation is characteristically practical: Dhanvantri is a physician; patients come to him. A vimana is therefore not required. This is one of the most distinctive Agamic exceptions in all of Srirangam.

Historical Evidence — The Inscription
The Hoysala-Era Foundation
In front of the shrine stands a stone slab bearing an inscription catalogued as ARE 80 of 1936-37 (Annual Report on Epigraphy). It dates from the third regnal year of the Hoysala king Pratapachakravartin Vira Ramanatha Deva (r. 1255–1295 CE) and records the following:
- **Patron:** Changadeva Singanna Dandanayaka, pradhani (chief minister) of the king
- **Custodian:** Garudavahana Pandita (Garudavahana Bhattar), the royal physician, designated as the rakshaka (protector) of the donor
- **What was founded:** A salai (hospital) to the west of the north gopuram in the fourth prakaram, with the enshrinement of the deity "Eduttakai-alagiya Nayinar"
- **Land endowment:** Agricultural land in Mummudisola Chaturvedimangalam, in Vilanadu, a subdivision of Pandikulasani Valanadu, was granted for the upkeep of the hospital
The inscription confirms that Garudavahana Bhattar — a practising Ayurvedic physician — established the Dhanvantri idol within the temple. This is not oral tradition; it is epigraphic record.

The Arogyashala — An Ancient Hospital
In earlier centuries, the Dhanvantri Sannidhi was not merely a place of worship. It functioned as the arogyashala — the hospital — for the residents of Srirangam. This is a significant detail for understanding the social role of South Indian temples: they were not only centres of worship and learning but also institutions of public health. The Dhanvantri shrine at Srirangam is physical evidence of this tradition.

Destruction and Rebuilding
The shrine was damaged during the devastating raids of Malik Kafur (1311 CE) and Ulugh Khan (1323 CE), during which much of the temple complex was plundered. It was subsequently rebuilt during the Nayak period. The painted ceilings and decorative elements visible today are attributed to the Nayak-era restoration.

Dhanvantri and Ranganatha — Special Rituals
The Kashayam — Divine Medical Service
One of the most charming traditions associated with this shrine dates from the time of Ramanuja (11th–12th century). Every day, before the aravanai — the final puja of the day when the Lord retires — a medicinal kashayam (herbal decoction) was sent from the Dhanvantri shrine to Lord Ranganatha.
The logic behind this practice is disarmingly simple: Ranganatha receives continuous offerings of very rich foods throughout the day — pongal, tamarind rice, sweet pongal, payasam, and many other preparations. To keep the Lord's system healthy after such a diet, Dhanvantri — as the resident physician — would dispatch a kashayam as his medical service to the Lord. A digestive made from ginger and jaggery was also sent to Ranganatha after certain meals.
This practice transforms an abstract theological concept — Dhanvantri as the physician of the gods — into a daily, tangible ritual within the temple's routine.

Herbal Theertham for Devotees
Visitors to the shrine receive a prasada theertham — a herbal decoction — as a sacred offering. This continues the ancient association between the shrine and healing, and distinguishes it from every other sannidhi in the complex, where the prasadam is invariably food or sanctified water.

Healing and Devotion
Devotees suffering from illness — particularly chronic or otherwise intractable ailments — come to this shrine to perform archana and thirumanjanam before Dhanvantri Perumal, praying for relief. It is a deeply held belief that worship here can cure bodily afflictions and restore health and vitality.
Dhanvantri Jayanti is celebrated annually on Dhanteras (Dhanvantri Trayodashi) — the thirteenth lunar day of Krishna Paksha in the month of Kartik, one day before Diwali. This day is also observed as National Ayurveda Day across India.

The Dhanvantri Sannidhi at Srirangam is not a minor sub-shrine. Founded in the 13th century by a Hoysala royal physician, functioning as a hospital for the temple town, possessing an Agamic architecture that breaks convention (no vimana, no utsavar), and maintaining a living tradition of herbal theertham and the kashayam service to Ranganatha — it stands as an unmatched institution among the 108 Divya Desams. In a temple complex often celebrated for its scale, its festivals and its theology, this small, elevated, tower-less shrine is quiet proof that the sacred and the medical were once the same thing.

The Deity
Garudalwar is enshrined here at a towering height of approximately 14 feet — a truly imposing presence. He sits directly opposite the Moolasthanam (main sanctum), in a seated posture with both hands joined in anjali (reverential salutation) facing the Lord.
He is adorned with the Ashta Nagabharanam — a jewelled ornament in the form of eight serpents. The hooded cobra that forms the base beneath him is carved with such lifelike intensity that it cannot fail to strike awe in the beholder. The image of Garuda seated upon serpents evokes the ancient Puranic relationship between Vishnu's mount and the Nagas — a tension between predator and prey that finds its resolution in the presence of the Lord.

The Legend — "Stay Right Here"
A beautiful sthala purana (local temple legend) explains why Garudalwar sits in this unusual posture.
Brahma once summoned Garuda and asked him to bring Lord Sri Ranganatha to this place and install him here. Garuda carried the Lord and set him down. Upon seeing Garuda, the Lord was pleased and commanded: "Stay right here."
From that day, Garudalwar has remained seated directly opposite the sanctum, waiting for the Lord's next command. It is said that he will continue to sit in this posture until Azhivemperuman (the Lord of Paramapada) issues a further order — an order that has never come. And so Garuda waits, in eternal devoted service, to this day.
This legend is offered as the explanation for Garudalwar's seated posture. In most other Divya Desams, Garuda appears standing, in flight, or kneeling. At Srirangam alone, he sits — not resting, but waiting. The distinction is theologically precise: his stillness is not repose but readiness, an unbroken act of obedience held across the centuries.

The Garuda Mandapam — Architectural Splendour
The Garuda Mandapam in which the Garudalwar sannidhi stands is one of the largest mandapams dedicated to Garuda among all 108 Divya Desams. With over 200 intricately carved pillars, it is widely regarded as one of the most beautiful halls in the entire Srirangam complex. The pillar carvings display fine sculptural workmanship and merit close examination by visitors.

Garuda in the Vaishnava Tradition
In the Sri Vaishnava sampradaya, Garuda is not merely Vishnu's mount — he is venerated as a divine devotee. The very name "Garudalwar" (with the honorific "Azhwar" meaning "one immersed in the divine") signals that he belongs to the same spiritual category as the great Azhwar saint-poets. He is Vishnu's nitya suri — an eternal attendant in Vaikuntha — who chose to serve as the Lord's vehicle out of love, not servitude.
At Srirangam, Garuda holds a special place of honour. When the Lord goes in procession, the Garuda Vahanam (Garuda mount) is among the most spectacular and eagerly awaited events, drawing large crowds of devotees. The image of Namperumal mounted upon Garuda, moving through the lamplit prakarams, is one of the defining sights of the temple's festival calendar.
Just as the temple elephant carrying Kaveri water at dawn evokes Gajendra Moksham (the liberation of the elephant-king by Vishnu), Garudalwar's eternal vigil opposite the sanctum represents the highest form of Vaishnava bhakti — patient, unwavering, and requiring no instruction beyond the Lord's single word.

Among the countless sacred shrines within the magnificent Sri Ranganathaswamy Temple at Srirangam, none commands greater affection from devotees than the shrine of Sri Ranga Nayaki Thayar, the eternal Divine Consort of Lord Sri Ranganatha. While Lord Ranganatha is revered as the Supreme Lord reclining on Adisesha, it is Sri Ranga Nayaki who embodies His infinite compassion, grace, and motherly protection. To generations of Sri Vaishnavas, she is not merely the Goddess Lakshmi but the compassionate Mother who intercedes on behalf of every devotee before the Lord.

The Sri Ranga Nayaki Temple is unique among all Divya Desams. Unlike many Vishnu temples where the Goddess shares the sanctum with the Lord, at Srirangam she has an independent and magnificent temple complex of her own, reflecting her supreme status within the Sri Vaishnava tradition. The shrine is one of the most sacred manifestations of Mahalakshmi and occupies a central place in the theology, rituals, festivals, and daily worship of Srirangam.

The Supreme Mother of Sri Vaishnavism

In Sri Vaishnava philosophy, salvation is possible through the combined grace of Divine Mother (Purushakara) and Lord Narayana. The Acharyas explain that while Lord Narayana is the Supreme Judge, Sri Mahalakshmi is the embodiment of mercy. She listens to the prayers of devotees, softens the Lord's justice with compassion, and recommends every sincere soul for His divine grace.

This theological principle reaches its highest expression at Srirangam.

Sri Vedanta Desika beautifully explains that no devotee should approach the Lord without first seeking the blessings of Thayar. Likewise, Sri Ramanuja consistently emphasized that surrender (Prapatti) becomes complete only through the compassionate mediation of Sri Mahalakshmi.

For this reason, countless devotees visit Sri Ranga Nayaki before or immediately after receiving darshan of Sri Ranganatha.

Why She is Called "Ranga Nayaki"

The name Ranga Nayaki literally means "Queen of Srirangam".

Ranga refers to Sri Rangam, the sacred abode of Lord Ranganatha.

Nayaki means Queen, Divine Mistress, or Supreme Lady.

She is regarded as the sovereign ruler of the spiritual kingdom of Bhooloka Vaikuntham. While Lord Ranganatha is the king, temple traditions often describe Sri Ranga Nayaki as the compassionate queen who governs with infinite mercy.

Even today many devotees affectionately address her as:

- Sri Ranga Nachiyar

- Periya Piratti

- Thayar

- Amma

- Mahalakshmi of Srirangam

The word "Thayar", meaning "Mother," perfectly reflects her relationship with devotees.

Historical Development of the Shrine

The shrine of Sri Ranga Nayaki evolved over several centuries under the patronage of the Cholas, Pandyas, Hoysalas, Vijayanagara rulers, and Nayak kings. Temple chronicles known as the Koil Olugu describe the growth of the temple, royal endowments, festivals, jewels, and ritual traditions connected with both Lord Ranganatha and Thayar.

Unlike many temples where the Goddess occupies a comparatively smaller shrine, Srirangam's architects created an independent temple for Thayar with its own sanctum, mandapas, processional routes, and festivals. This architectural choice reflects the elevated theological status accorded to Mahalakshmi within Sri Vaishnavism.

Several inscriptions record donations of gold ornaments, silk garments, lamps, lands, and villages dedicated specifically for the worship of Sri Ranga Nayaki.

The Unique Tradition – Thayar Rarely Leaves Her Temple

One of the most fascinating traditions at Srirangam is that Sri Ranga Nayaki almost never leaves her sanctum.

Unlike Namperumal (the processional form of Lord Ranganatha), who participates in numerous festivals throughout the year, Thayar generally remains within her shrine. This practice symbolizes the dignity and majesty of the Divine Queen.

During important festivals, the Lord Himself comes to visit His Divine Consort rather than the Goddess travelling outside.

This beautiful tradition teaches that while the Lord actively seeks His devotees, the Divine Mother remains the ever-compassionate refuge where every soul may approach without hesitation.

Panguni Uttiram – The Divine Wedding

The grandest festival associated with Sri Ranga Nayaki is Panguni Uttiram, celebrated during the Tamil month of Panguni (March–April).

This festival commemorates the celestial wedding of Lord Ranganatha and Sri Ranga Nayaki. It is among the most emotionally moving celebrations in the temple calendar.

Thousands of devotees gather to witness the sacred union, believing that participation brings harmony in marriage, family happiness, prosperity, and spiritual blessings.

The festival also symbolizes the inseparable union of Sri (Lakshmi) and Narayana, representing compassion and justice, grace and protection, divine love and cosmic order.

The Koil Olugu and temple traditions preserve detailed accounts of these ceremonies and their historical continuity across centuries.

Iconography of Sri Ranga Nayaki

The presiding deity appears seated in majestic posture, radiating serenity and compassion.

She is richly adorned with:

- Golden crown

- Exquisite silk garments

- Magnificent diamond and emerald jewellery

- Lotus in her hand

- Abhaya Mudra (gesture of protection)

- Varada Mudra (gesture of granting boons)

Her gentle smile is often described by devotees as one of the most compassionate expressions found in any temple.

Unlike fierce manifestations of the Divine Mother found elsewhere, Sri Ranga Nayaki represents pure maternal affection, forgiveness, patience, and unconditional love.

The Spiritual Significance of Thayar

Sri Vaishnava philosophy identifies Mahalakshmi with six principal qualities:

- Compassion

- Mercy

- Prosperity

- Divine Wisdom

- Protection

- Mediation before the Lord

Acharyas explain that even when devotees feel unworthy of approaching the Supreme Lord, they may confidently approach Thayar, who never rejects anyone.

This doctrine of Purushakara is one of the defining theological contributions of the Sri Vaishnava tradition and is deeply associated with Srirangam.

Sri Ramanuja and Ranga Nayaki

Sri Ramanuja regarded Srirangam as the spiritual capital of Sri Vaishnavism. During his long residence here, he systematized temple worship, festivals, and administrative practices.

The daily rituals established under his guidance ensured that worship of Sri Ranga Nayaki received equal prominence alongside Lord Ranganatha. His reforms profoundly influenced temple life and are reflected in later temple records.

Darshan Experience

Pilgrims often describe the atmosphere within the Thayar shrine as remarkably peaceful.

Compared to the larger crowds at the sanctum of Lord Ranganatha, the shrine of Sri Ranga Nayaki offers devotees a quieter space for prayer and contemplation. Families frequently pray here for marriage, childbirth, health, education, and domestic harmony, reflecting her enduring role as the compassionate Mother.

Traditional devotees often recommend completing one's pilgrimage with the blessings of Thayar, believing that her grace makes the Lord's blessings complete.

Architectural Features

- The temple dedicated to Sri Ranga Nayaki contains:

- Independent sanctum (Garbhagriha)

- Maha Mandapa

- Beautiful pillared halls

- Ornate Vijayanagara and Nayak sculptures

- Festival mandapas

- Processional pathways

- Intricate stone carvings and traditional iconography

The shrine integrates seamlessly into the vast sacred city of Srirangam while preserving its own identity as the abode of the Divine Mother.

Living Legacy

For nearly a thousand years, Sri Ranga Nayaki has remained the spiritual mother of millions of devotees. Kings sought her blessings before battles, Acharyas composed hymns in her praise, saints surrendered at her feet, and ordinary devotees continue to experience her compassion in their daily lives.

In the hearts of Sri Vaishnavas, no pilgrimage to Srirangam is complete without standing before Sri Ranga Nayaki Thayar, offering a silent prayer, and receiving the reassuring glance of the Divine Mother. Her shrine stands as a timeless reminder that divine grace is inseparable from divine compassion, and that every seeker—regardless of background or circumstance—is welcomed by the Mother before being embraced by the Lord.
